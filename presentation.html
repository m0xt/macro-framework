The Problem

Macro indicators are everywhere. VIX, credit spreads, yield curves, breadth, liquidity, jobless claims, shipping indices...

The challenge isn't finding data. It's that most frameworks collapse it into a single answer — either telling you when to act, or where you are in the cycle. Almost never both.

Momentum signals tell you to act — but not whether the action has wind at its back.

Cycle readings tell you where you are — but not whether to do anything about it.

What we built:

  • Two pillars, paired — a velocity signal and a position reading, designed to condition each other
  • MRMI — 3 regime indicators at different speeds, combined into one weighted composite
  • Macro Seasons — growth × inflation quadrant for cycle context
  • Optimized via grid search with out-of-sample validation
  • Single dashboard with clear hierarchy: decision → drivers → context

The Framework: Two Questions

Knowing whether momentum is positive isn't enough. You also need to know where that momentum is starting from.

Question 1 — Velocity

Is the wind at my back right now?

Are leading indicators pointing up or down today? This is the trade-or-don't-trade decision. Updates daily, drives the binary risk-on / risk-off call.

Answered by → Milk Road Momentum Index (MRMI)

Question 2 — Position

Where in the cycle am I starting from?

Is the economy deep in contraction, mid-expansion, or running hot? This conditions what the velocity signal actually means.

Answered by → Macro Seasons

Why both: A green MRMI signal coming out of cycle Winter is early-cycle — the regime can run for months and the upside is asymmetric. The same green signal at cycle peak is late-cycle — shorter runway, smaller upside, fatter left tail. Same number, different meaning.

Pillar 1 — Velocity

The Milk Road Momentum Index (MRMI)

A daily composite that answers "Is the wind at my back right now?" Three regime indicators, each measuring a different force at a different speed, normalized to a common scale and combined into one weighted signal.

IndicatorWhat It CapturesWindowSpeedWeight
Growth Impulses (GII) Momentum across credit, sectors, copper, vol, yield curve, shipping 21d ROC, 2yr z-score Fast 37%
Sector Breadth Whether the rally is broad (cyclicals participating) or narrow 63d z-score Medium 35%
Financial Conditions Equity vol + bond vol + credit spread = market stress 252d z-score Slow 28%

What's a z-score? A z-score expresses today's value as standard deviations from its recent typical range. +2 = unusually high (above ~98% of recent observations). 0 = right at average. −2 = unusually low. This puts copper prices, VIX levels, and credit spreads — all on totally different scales — onto one comparable axis. The "window" column above is how far back we look to define "recent typical."

How the signal is produced

Each indicator outputs a z-scored composite. The weighted average (37 / 35 / 28) crosses a threshold → RISK-ON or RISK-OFF. One number, one color, one action.

Why three, not one

Three time horizons of market signaling: fast (positioning shifts), medium (rotation breadth), slow (structural stress). When fast indicators give false signals, the slow ones stabilize the composite — and vice versa.

Why These Three Indicators?

What We Tested

840+ parameter combinations per indicator. Grid search across lookback periods, smoothing, thresholds, component subsets. 70/30 in-sample / out-of-sample split.

What We Dropped

  • IG credit spread — adding it to Financial Conditions didn't improve the signal
  • SLX (Steel ETF) — noisy, hurt Sector Breadth performance
  • Baltic Dry Index — negative alpha out-of-sample across all assets
  • GII "both positive" mode — green only 5% of the time, too restrictive

What Survived

  • GII fast ROC (21d) — shorter than original (42d), catches turns faster
  • FinCon (252d) — longer than original (126d), more structural
  • Breadth (63d) — shorter than original (252d), more responsive
  • No smoothing — EMA consistently hurt performance across all indicators

The composite outperforms every individual indicator because the three capture different things at different speeds. FinCon misses BTC entirely. GII is noisy for SPX alone. Breadth is the strongest standalone but combining all three reduces drawdowns further.

Pillar 2 — Position

Macro Seasons

A slow-moving context layer that answers "Where in the cycle are we right now?" Two dimensions — growth and inflation — locate the economy in one of four regimes. Not a trading signal: it conditions what the MRMI signal means.

AxisWhat It MeasuresInputsReading
Y — Growth Real-economy growth vs long-run trend Real GDP YoY, z-scored over 10y Above 0 = above trend (running hot) · Below 0 = below trend (running cold)
X — Inflation Price pressure vs the Fed's 2% target (Core CPI YoY − 2%) ÷ 10y std dev of Core CPI Right of 0 = above target · Left of 0 = below target

The Four Quadrants

Spring (Growth ↑ + Inflation ↓)

Growth without overheating. The Fed can stay accommodative while the economy expands.

Summer (Growth ↑ + Inflation ↑)

Strong demand pushing prices up. Fed will eventually tighten, but cycle still expanding.

Winter (Growth ↓ + Inflation ↓)

Slowing economy with falling prices. Fed will eventually pivot to easing.

Fall (Growth ↓ + Inflation ↑)

The worst combination — economy weakening but Fed can't ease because of inflation.

How it conditions MRMI

MRMI green + cycle expanding = economy-driven rally (lean into cyclicals). MRMI green + cycle contracting = liquidity- or narrative-driven rally (favor quality, stay selective). Same signal, different setup.

Why it changes slowly

Real-economy data is reported monthly with publication lags. The cycle moves on quarters, not days. That's a feature: it sets the strategic backdrop, while MRMI handles the tactical decision.

Why a library, not more pillars

A simple two-question framework drives clean decisions. The library lives alongside — visible, interpretable, but not voting on the binary call. Add when useful, never to add.

When something gets promoted

If a library indicator repeatedly leads or contradicts the formal axes at meaningful turns — and adding it improves interpretive power without bloating the framework — it moves into MRMI or Macro Seasons.

How to Use It

Open the dashboard. Read it top-down. The layout mirrors the framework — every glance answers the questions in the same order.

The Read · top to bottom

STEP 1

The banner

RISK-ON or RISK-OFF? Plus the current quadrant badge. If both unchanged from yesterday, you're done in 30 seconds.

STEP 2

MRMI drivers

Which of GII / Breadth / FinCon is moving? A divergence — one driver out of step — is worth a closer look.

STEP 3

Macro Seasons

Where's the dot? Drifting toward a new quadrant? A quadrant change reframes the playbook even if MRMI hasn't flipped.

STEP 4

The library

Anything unusual? M2 contracting? ISM falling? Library entries explain narrative shifts but don't drive the call.

The Decision · what each move means

  • MRMI flips green → risk-on, with sizing informed by where the cycle dot sits
  • MRMI flips red → reduce exposure, even if the cycle reading looks supportive
  • Cycle drifts to a new quadrant → the interpretation of the same MRMI signal changes
  • Library divergence → context for the narrative, not a trigger for action

The Cadence · time per session

  • Daily (30 sec) — banner + driver scan
  • Weekly (5 min) — Macro Seasons drift, library check, sub-component divergences
  • Monthly (30 min) — full framework review, macro report, evaluate library candidates for promotion
  • Annually — re-optimize MRMI parameters, reassess inflation/growth lookbacks

What happens with execution delay and trading friction?

The Key Finding

With same-day execution, alpha is robust even with 50bps friction (+4.8%).

But a 1-day delay kills the edge entirely. Alpha drops from +13.3% to -2.1%.

Implication

This signal requires daily monitoring and same-day action. Short regime durations (~17 days) mean the first day matters. Most of the alpha comes from catching regime transitions immediately.

This is a feature of fast signals. Slower signals (like 200d SMA) are more forgiving of delay but generate less alpha.

What It Doesn't Do

Limitations

  • Not a crystal ball — it reacts to conditions, doesn't predict them. Lag is inherent.
  • ~15 flips/year, same-day execution required — a 1-day delay erases the alpha entirely (tested). Short regimes (~17 days) demand daily monitoring.
  • Walk-forward tested 2021–2026 only — 6 years, all positive. But no data before 2016 means we can't test 2008 or dot-com era.
  • No position sizing — it's binary (in/out), not graduated. A more sophisticated approach would scale exposure by signal strength.
  • No asset allocation — tells you risk-on vs risk-off, not which assets to favor within each regime.

What It Does Well

  • Drawdown protection — SPX max drawdown from -18.9% to -2.9% out-of-sample. This alone justifies the framework.
  • Works across asset classes — positive alpha on SPX, IWM, and BTC simultaneously. Positive SPX alpha every year in walk-forward (2021–2026).
  • Simple decision — one number, one color, one action. No ambiguity.
  • Transparent — every component is visible in the scorecard. You can see exactly what's driving the signal.
  • Free data — no expensive subscriptions needed. Yahoo Finance + FRED.
