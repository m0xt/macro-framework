The macro stress score is sitting at 0.82 — squarely in the Calm range — and the buffer it feeds into the MRMI headline signal is nearly at full tailwind strength (0.46 out of a possible 0.50). That is a permissive setup for risk exposure, and right now the data mostly support it. The real-economy axis is the one soft spot: the Real Economy Score is at −0.67, meaning the composite picture of spending, jobs, income, and growth is running slightly below trend, weighed down by consumer sentiment hitting an [all-time low in the University of Michigan's May survey](https://www.spglobal.com/ratings/en/regulatory/article/consumer-pulse-us-consumers-resilience-could-be-tested-across-sectors-s101675447) and nagging uncertainty about tariff costs feeding through to household budgets. Hard data, however, is telling a different story: [April retail sales rose 0.5% month-over-month and 4.9% year-over-year](https://www.census.gov/retail/marts/www/marts_current.pdf), and the Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model [pushed its Q2 estimate up to 4.3% on May 21](https://www.atlantafed.org/-/media/Project/Atlanta/FRBA/Documents/cqer/researchcq/gdpnow/RealGDPTrackingSlides.pdf), with consumption and investment tracking better than the prior week — that GDPNow contribution is what keeps the Real Economy Score from falling further. On inflation, the 6-month direction is barely positive at +0.04 percentage points, and with core CPI at 2.74% YoY the acceleration is not yet showing up in the hard numbers, but the risk is real: [core PCE climbed to 3.2% in March](https://www.clevelandfed.org/indicators-and-data/inflation-nowcasting) and top forecasters are [projecting headline PCE near 4.5% for Q2](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/15/inflation-rate-projected-to-hit-6percent-in-the-second-quarter-top-economic-forecasters.html) as tariff costs that importers have been absorbing start reaching shelf prices. The [Moody's Aaa→Aa1 downgrade on May 16](https://ratings.moodys.com/ratings-news/443154) is not a direct stress driver in this framework, but it is reinforcing upward pressure on the 10-year real rate (now 2.18%) and the 5-year breakeven (2.54%), which tightens financial conditions at the margin. What would materially shift the stress score higher is a combination of tariff pass-through landing in the next core CPI or PCE print — reversing the flat 6-month inflation direction — while the hard spending and jobs data follow sentiment lower; either alone is tolerable, but both together would push the score toward Watch and begin eroding the macro buffer that is currently keeping MRMI at full allocation.
