The stress score sits at +0.51 (7d -0.08), barely changed and confirmed Calm, held there by an inflation picture that cooperated and a real economy that is muddling through rather than falling. Inflation direction is unchanged at +0.00 (7d +0.00): the [June CPI report released July 14](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/14/consumer-price-index-inflation-report-june-2026.html) showed core prices flat on the month with the 12-month rate at 2.60%, well below the 0.3% monthly consensus, confirming that the tariff pass-through the New York Fed flagged as "still in the pipeline" did not land in June goods or services prices. On the real economy side, the score is -0.68 (7d +0.11) — slightly better but still negative — and new data this week supports a slow-but-intact consumer: [June retail sales rose 0.2%](https://qz.com/us-retail-sales-june-2026-071626), a touch below the 0.3% forecast, but the miss was largely a gasoline-price drag, and in real terms spending was up 0.6%. [Housing starts jumped 19% in June to 1.43 million annualized](https://investinglive.com/news/us-housing-starts-for-june-1-427-million-versus-1-310-million-estimate), lifting [GDPNow to 1.7%](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow) for Q2 and partly reversing the early-July slide from 1.3%, but the entire gain came from apartments — single-family starts fell to an eight-month low, aligning with the pending home sales softness already in the picture. With neither axis pushing harder, the macro buffer holds at +0.47 and stress has no obvious near-term trigger. Two prints will determine whether this holds: Q2 GDP on July 30, where GDPNow at 1.7% still leaves room for a downside surprise that would push the real economy score deeper negative, and the July CPI on August 12, where deferred tariff pass-through gets its first real test.
