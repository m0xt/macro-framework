The macro stress score is +1.23 — deep in the Calm zone (0–3) — and has nudged only +0.37 higher this week, leaving the macro buffer at +0.44, which is close to full strength as a tailwind for the headline MRMI signal. Growth is the softer side of the picture: the Real Economy Score is -1.02 and fell another -0.31 over seven days, driven largely by the Atlanta Fed's [GDPNow tracker retreating to 3.0% for Q2](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow) (down from 3.8% earlier in the quarter) and consumers clearly pulling back on big-ticket items — April retail data showed declines in furniture, auto dealerships, and department stores. That said, this morning's [May payrolls report](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm) came in at +172,000, more than double the ~80,000 consensus, with prior months revised up a combined 93,000 — and since that data likely didn't make it into today's Real Economy Score, the next dashboard build should partially offset the recent slide. Inflation is essentially inert right now: the six-month change in core CPI sits at +0.04 pp, nearly flat, and the 2.74% year-over-year level is still above the Fed's target but has stopped accelerating over the measured window. The next real test is the [May CPI release on June 10](https://www.bls.gov/cpi/) — April printed a hot 0.4% monthly gain, and a repeat would push the Inflation Direction delta meaningfully higher and start eroding the stress score's calm read; a cooler number would confirm the current picture and keep the macro buffer near full.
