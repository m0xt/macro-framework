The trouble is that both the market signal and the macro insulation hinge on the same 90-day clock: [Dallas Fed research](https://www.dallasfed.org/research/economics/2026/0505-mau) suggests tariff effects on PCE prices peaked in Q1, but Goldman's [estimated one percentage point of incremental inflation](https://www.morningstar.com/economy/inflation-set-rise-tariff-costs-hit-consumers-2026) from tariff pass-through hasn't fully cleared the pipeline, and if core CPI's six-month drift reverses into positive territory over the next two to three months — concurrent with the labor market softening past the −1σ threshold our real economy score is currently approaching — the buffer compresses rapidly. On the market side, breadth's -0.655 weekly move tells you the rally has already priced the truce without broad cyclical confirmation; the [S&P's 14% surge in 23 sessions](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/small-caps-steal-the-spotlight-as-sp-500-rally-masks-weak-breadth-4654838) is a sentiment event, not a participation event, and MMI won't sustainably advance until semis, transports, homebuilders, and regional banks re-engage rather than fade. The two pillars are therefore reading the same macro moment from opposite angles: markets are celebrating a trade ceasefire while the economy quietly absorbs the lagged costs of the tariff regime that preceded it. Stay LONG — the buffer is doing exactly what it's designed to do — but the asymmetric risk is that the 90-day truce resolves the market's uncertainty without resolving the inflation pipeline, leaving the framework exposed to a stress trigger just as MMI breadth needs to carry more weight.
