The stress score sits at +1.18, squarely in the Calm band, and the macro buffer it drives into MRMI is at +0.44 — near its full tailwind. That reading reflects a genuine alignment: inflation is barely moving (core CPI at 2.74% year-on-year with a 6-month directional change of just +0.04 pp), contributing almost nothing to stress, while growth is soft but not deteriorating. The Real Economy Score is at -0.98 — the broad composite of spending, jobs, income, and the nowcast running below historical norms — but it improved +0.10 on the week, and Friday's [May jobs report](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm) tells the story: 172,000 nonfarm payrolls against an 80,000 consensus, plus a combined 93,000 upward revision to March and April, meaning the labor market was quietly stronger than it looked, with wage growth at 3.4% year-on-year too soft to rekindle price pressure. The catch is composition — roughly three-quarters of May's gain came from leisure and hospitality and local government, the cyclically softer corners of the economy — which is why the Real Economy Score hasn't flipped positive despite the headline beat, and why the [Atlanta Fed's Q2 GDPNow has slipped from 3.8% to 3.0%](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow) over the past week. Tariff pass-through remains a slow-moving headwind, but with core inflation nearly flat on a 6-month basis there is no sign yet that it is reaccelerating in a way that would move the stress score. The view changes most directly on June 10, when May CPI lands: a clear re-acceleration from the current near-flat trajectory would push the inflation direction axis meaningfully positive, erode the macro buffer, and start lifting stress out of Calm; a second tame print would confirm the current regime holds.
