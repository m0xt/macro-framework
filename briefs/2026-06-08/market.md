MMI sits at +0.25 — exactly on the LONG/CAUTION line — after a 7-day decline of -0.31, and nearly all of that move came from one source: financial conditions. [Friday's May payrolls print](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-05/us-adds-172-000-jobs-in-may-beating-all-economists-estimates) — 172,000 jobs versus an 85,000 forecast, with prior months revised up by a combined 93,000 — sent [10-year yields above 4.53%](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/05/treasury-yields-ease-as-traders-await-key-labor-market-data-.html) and shifted rate-hike odds to 70% by year-end, pulling financial conditions down -0.79 over the week to -0.11. What stopped the bleed is that equity markets didn't flinch: VIX held at 18.80, bond volatility (MOVE at 75.20) stayed contained, and high-yield spreads remain near historically tight levels around 2.74%, which is why the growth impulse index snapped back a sharp +0.43 on the day to land at +0.13. Breadth is the strongest component at +0.73, down only -0.13 on the week — the rally is getting broad support, helped along by a [rotation into energy, materials, and industrials](https://moneyflows.com/blog/2026-market-rotation-leaves-the-sp-500-in-the-dust/) running alongside the AI-led tech gains. The core tension is between the bond market, which is repricing for an economy that won't cool, and equity markets, which are treating tight spreads and healthy breadth as confirmation the growth picture is still fine. May CPI on June 10 and the [June 17 FOMC dot plot](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomccalendars.htm) are the next hard tests: another hot inflation read or an explicit hawkish signal from the Fed would pressure financial conditions further and could tip MMI out of its current green hold.
