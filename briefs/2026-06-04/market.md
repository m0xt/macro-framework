The market momentum index (MMI) is still green at +0.333, but a -0.418 drop over the past seven days is the dashboard's clearest signal that the post-March recovery trade has run into a ceiling. The biggest driver of that fade is Financial Conditions, which shed -0.501 on the week: with [high-yield spreads near 2007-era tights and investment grade approaching 1998 lows](https://www.stonex.com/en/insights/credit-spreads-tighten-as-risk-appetite-returns-fast/), VIX sitting at 16.6, and bond vol (MOVE) at 73.6, conditions are benign — but they've compressed about as far as they can go, so the tailwind from further tightening has essentially stalled. The Growth Impulse sub-index tells a related story: it's barely positive at +0.015 after dropping -0.346, because credit spreads at multi-decade tights carry little incremental signal and other growth proxies like copper and sector rotation haven't filled the gap. Breadth is the strongest component at +0.740, but it dropped -0.407 on the week too, which maps directly onto what the market data shows: the [S&P 500 closed above 7,600 for the first time on June 2](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/01/stock-market-today-live-updates.html) after a 16% two-month surge, yet only about 52% of its members trade above their 50-day moving average, with gains overwhelmingly concentrated in AI and tech mega-caps — [strip out AI-linked names and the S&P's recent run effectively disappears](https://cryptobriefing.com/sp500-ai-stocks-142-percent-gain-concentration/). The dashboard is not flashing a warning — MMI remains positive and momentum is intact — but the read is that the market is printing record highs on a narrowing foundation, which is how fades begin rather than how they end. What would re-accelerate the score: breadth widens as cyclicals and smaller names join the rally, or fresh data re-fires the growth impulse; what would break it is a blowout in credit spreads, a VIX reset above 20, or continued narrowing in participation until breadth drops toward neutral.
