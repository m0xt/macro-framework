The macro stress pressure score is sitting near zero — squarely in ordinary historical territory — because the two inputs that would compound into a stress signal are pulling in opposite directions rather than reinforcing each other. The real economy axis is the soft half of that pair: the composite of PCE, the Sahm indicator, real personal income, and GDPNow lands at −0.94σ, reflecting a labor market that is cooling (March unemployment at 4.3%, with the [April payrolls print of 177K](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/april-jobs-report-shows-us-labor-market-remained-resilient-in-wake-of-liberation-day-tariff-announcement-123204519.html) offering a short-term reprieve that precedes, rather than reflects, tariff disruption), alongside a [Q1 GDP advance of only +2.0%](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/gdp-advance-estimate-1st-quarter-2026) that missed consensus and was padded by front-loaded imports businesses were rushing in ahead of "Liberation Day" levies. The inflation axis, however, is running the other direction: [core CPI sits at 2.60% YoY](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm) and the six-month trend is down roughly 0.4 percentage points, meaning price pressure has been softening — not re-accelerating — over the window the model measures, which is why the AND-gate that drives hard stress remains closed and the macro buffer feeding MRMI stays at full strength. The bright spot within the growth composite is [Atlanta Fed GDPNow tracking Q2 at 3.7%](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow), which is doing real work to keep the real economy score from deteriorating further; strip that out and the underlying fundamental trio (PCE, Sahm, real income) tells a more vulnerable story. The scenario that would push the pressure score materially higher — and begin eroding that buffer — is one where tariff pass-through reverses the disinflationary drift in core CPI while the real economy composite stays anchored below zero: the [FOMC's 8-4 hold at 3.5–3.75%](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/29/fed-interest-rate-decision-april-2026.html) signals the committee itself is watching that exact risk, caught between a labor market that has yet to fully price in import-cost shocks and an inflation trend that could turn on a quarter's worth of tariff data.
