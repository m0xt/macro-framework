The stress score's sharp 7d drop of -2.61 lands it at +0.51, firmly in Calm, and the engine behind that move was not the economy getting better but inflation getting less threatening: the six-month trend in core CPI eased from moderately accelerating to flat, with inflation direction now at +0.00 (7d -0.30), stripping away the biggest single contributor to stress and holding the macro buffer at +0.47. On the real economy side, the score remains in negative territory at -0.68, still dragged by June's payroll shortfall, but the week's best read on the labor market gave it a nudge: [initial jobless claims fell to a 10-week low of 208,000](https://yournews.com/2026/07/16/7112714/u-s-jobless-claims-fall-to-10-week-low-as-labor-market/) for the week ending July 11, well below the 219,000 consensus, confirming that layoffs remain contained even as hiring stays slow. Against that, two of the better forward-looking indicators came in soft: [pending home sales fell 5.4% in June](https://www.advisorperspectives.com/dshort/updates/2026/07/16/pending-home-sales-june-2026), pointing to a weaker resale market ahead, and the [Conference Board Leading Economic Index fell 0.2% in June](https://finance.yahoo.com/economy/articles/conference-board-leading-economic-index-140000348.html), partially reversing May's gain, with weakness in consumer expectations and building permits as the main drags — the broad picture is that last week's Philly Fed manufacturing surge has not yet shown up in the leading indicators. The tariff pass-through risk flagged last week has not resolved: with inflation direction sitting exactly at +0.00, core CPI at 2.60%, and manufacturer input costs still elevated, any re-acceleration in goods or services prices would flip that reading positive and rebuild stress toward Watch quickly. Q2 GDP on July 30 and the July CPI on August 12 are the two prints that would materially move this view in either direction.
