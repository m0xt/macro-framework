The macro stress score eased to +3.13 this week — down -0.27 from +3.39 — but the improvement is narrow and the reading remains in Watch territory, the same band that has held since the June payrolls shock. The prior brief's key test, today's [June inflation report](https://www.bls.gov/cpi/), landed broadly in line with pre-release consensus: headline inflation almost certainly fell sharply on energy price relief from the US-Iran ceasefire, but that is not what the framework tracks — the six-month change in core inflation held flat at +0.30, meaning underlying price pressure neither accelerated nor retreated, and the worst-case scenario flagged last week — growth weakening and core inflation speeding up simultaneously — did not materialize. The growth side showed a thin improvement: the Real Economy Score rose to -0.79 from -0.87, reflecting [initial claims for the week ending July 4 printing at 215,000](https://www.breitbart.com/economy/2026/07/09/jobless-claims-dip-unexpectedly-to-215000/) against a 219,000 forecast, and a [GDPNow Q2 estimate that steadied at 1.3% annualized as of July 8](https://www.tradingview.com/news/macenews:d4e77231e094b:0-us-atlanta-fed-gdpnow-model-estimates-us-q2-gdp-growth-rate-at-1-3-down-from-1-4-after-latest-economic-data-including-trade-balance/) after its plunge from 2.5% two weeks earlier — a pause in the deterioration, not a turn. The [June payrolls miss](https://www.cnbc.com/amp/2026/07/02/jobs-report-june-2026-.html) — 57,000 jobs against a 115,000 consensus, with prior months revised down another 74,000 combined — remains the structural weight keeping the growth score negative, and this week's improvement is modest enough that a single poor claims print would reverse it. The macro buffer ticked to +0.34 from +0.33, barely changed and still more than a third eroded from its full-strength ceiling of +0.50. The clearest path to a meaningfully lower stress score now runs through the advance Q2 GDP report on July 30 and next month's inflation print: GDP confirming above 1.5% and core's six-month trend beginning to flatten would ease both axes at once; if instead [continuing claims](https://verifiedinvesting.com/blogs/us-economic-metrics/jobless-claims-week-july-4-2026) — already drifting to 1,814,000, their highest since late March — push materially higher while tariff costs reignite core prices, the framework would be tracking toward its highest stress readings of the year.
