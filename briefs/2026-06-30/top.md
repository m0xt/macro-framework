The headline allocation posture crossed into LONG this week, rising from +0.12 to +0.30, and it did so in the scenario last week's brief flagged as the one most likely to prevent it: [May PCE came in at 4.1% headline and 3.4% core](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/25/pce-inflation-report-may-2026-.html), the hottest inflation reading in three years and above the Fed's own freshly-raised forecast. The market absorbed it without a volatility event — breadth surged to +1.95, VIX fell to 17.65, and market momentum reached +0.65 — and that wide participation is specifically what carried the posture above the +0.25 threshold for full exposure: when equities rally broadly rather than narrowly, the breadth reading builds enough cushion for market momentum to stay elevated even as financial conditions weaken. The economy pillar is more reserved: stress ticked to +1.97 and the macro buffer held flat at +0.40 while GDPNow slowed to 2.5% annualized for the second quarter as real spending missed — below-trend growth alongside above-target inflation is the combination that limits how far the macro buffer can recover, and a recovering macro buffer is what would make the current LONG call more durable. The dashboard has not yet registered the full weight of that inflation story: the inflation axis tracks core CPI, not PCE, and core CPI held at 2.90% this week, leaving the reading flat at +0.30 — core PCE running at 3.4% means the stress score will face additional upside pressure if CPI starts to follow, eroding the macro buffer and squeezing the posture back toward CAUTION. The two developments that matter most from here are the ones both pillar authors identified: technology weakness spreading to the sectors that drove breadth's surge, and the [July 14 CPI release](https://www.bls.gov/schedule/news_release/cpi.htm) printing above 2.90% — individually they stress-test the posture, together they could move it.
