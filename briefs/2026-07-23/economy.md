The stress score holds at +0.50 — ticking one basis point lower from last week's +0.51 and confirmed Calm, with neither underlying axis generating new pressure. The headline is effectively unchanged: inflation is not pushing, and the real economy is muddling along at -0.67 (7d +0.11), just slightly less negative than a week ago. The macro buffer stays at +0.47, meaning headline posture retains most of its tailwind.

The most important new data this week came from the [S&P Global flash PMI for July](https://www.pmi.spglobal.com/Public/Home/PressRelease/0f8762edff954b619d90d12531497919): the composite index jumped to 54.6 from 52.9, driven entirely by services surging to 55.2, and S&P Global's chief economist flagged the reading as consistent with GDP expanding at a 2.3% annualized rate to start the third quarter — well above where Q2 is tracking. Manufacturing slipped to 49.5 and is in outright contraction, a pattern that has persisted, but services employ the bulk of U.S. workers and drive the spending data that feeds the real economy score most directly. That split is why the score remains slightly negative — manufacturing drag is real — but the services strength keeps it from deteriorating further. [Weekly initial jobless claims for the week ending July 11](https://www.indexbox.io/blog/weekly-jobless-claims-fall-to-208000-down-8000-from-prior-week/) dropped to 208,000, the lowest print in over two months, reinforcing that layoffs remain contained even as hiring is not booming.

On inflation, the picture has not shifted. The inflation direction reading holds at +0.00 (7d +0.00), with core CPI anchored at 2.60% year-over-year — the tariff pass-through the New York Fed had flagged as "still in the pipeline" has not shown up in the data yet, and [research from the Dallas Fed](https://www.dallasfed.org/research/economics/2026/0505-mau) and [Minneapolis Fed](https://www.minneapolisfed.org/article/2026/tariffs-cant-explain-rising-goods-inflation) suggests most of the tariff-driven goods price pressure peaked in early 2026 and is now fading. Two watch-points from last week's brief remain open: Q2 GDP on July 30, where [GDPNow at 1.7%](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow) leaves meaningful room for a downside miss that would push the real economy score deeper negative, and July CPI on August 12, the first real test of whether deferred tariff costs start landing in services. The flash PMI data suggest Q3 is starting on firmer footing than Q2 finished, which is modestly encouraging — but the stress score will not move until hard data, not surveys, confirms it.
