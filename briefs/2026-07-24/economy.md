The stress score eased to +0.49 this week — a -0.04 move over seven days that keeps it firmly in the Calm range and leaves the macro buffer essentially intact at +0.48. The week's most decisive new data came from [Thursday's jobless claims](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-23/us-initial-jobless-claims-fall-to-lowest-level-since-1969): initial filings for the week ending July 18 fell to 187,000, down 22,000 from the prior week and the lowest reading since September 1969, a full 25,000 below the 212,000 consensus — a result that flatly contradicts any narrative of a labor market buckling under tariff pressure. That strength is beginning to show in the real economy score, which ticked to -0.66 (7d +0.05), its least-negative reading in several weeks, though [GDPNow](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow) holding at 1.7% annualized keeps output soft and the score below zero ahead of the July 30 advance estimate. Inflation direction is unchanged at +0.00 (7d +0.00), core CPI still at 2.60% year-over-year, with no new price data landing this week. Both watch-points from last week remain open: Q2 GDP on July 30, where a print materially below 1.5% annualized would push the real economy score lower and begin eroding the macro buffer; and July CPI on August 12, where [forecasters now expect](https://www.morningstar.com/economy/july-cpi-report-forecasts-show-continued-tariff-driven-inflation) core to rise 0.3% on the month and 3.0% year-over-year as 301 tariffs that took effect in July start passing through to goods prices. If that inflation call lands and GDP disappoints simultaneously, the direction axis would finally turn positive, stress would push toward the Watch range, and the macro buffer would begin to erode — but this week's labor data is the strongest single argument yet that the real economy is holding up better than the output trackers suggest.
