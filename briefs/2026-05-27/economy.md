The economy pillar's stress score sits at **0.8 out of 10** — firmly in the Calm zone and essentially as benign as the framework gets. That reading delivers a macro buffer of 0.46, close to the maximum 0.5 tailwind, which means the MRMI is drawing near-full support from the economy side rather than facing any erosion. What keeps the score so low is a combination of soft-but-not-breaking growth and inflation that is barely drifting higher on the smoothed measure that actually drives the model.

On growth, the Real Economy Score sits at **-0.66 standard deviations below its historical norm**, reflecting a labor market that is cooling rather than healthy: April payrolls came in at [+115,000](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/jobless-claims) — solid enough to avoid alarm but well below the pace seen in early 2026 — and [consumer confidence slipped to 93.1 in May](https://www.conference-board.org/topics/consumer-confidence/), with two-thirds of surveyed households citing spending cutbacks due to higher prices. The positive offset is that jobless claims remain low (209,000 in the week ending May 16), the unemployment rate is steady at 4.3%, and GDPNow is tracking in positive territory for Q2, which is why the growth sub-score improved a small +0.10 week-over-week. On inflation, the picture is where the real tension lives: [April core CPI printed +0.4% month-over-month, lifting the year-over-year rate from 2.6% to 2.8%](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/archives/cpi_05122026.htm), the sharpest monthly read in several months and widely attributed to [tariff pass-through beginning to land in goods prices](https://economy.ac/review/2026/05/202605289187). That jump does not yet register loudly in the framework because our inflation direction signal measures the **six-month change in core CPI year-over-year** — currently only +0.04 percentage points — so a single hot month is absorbed rather than amplified. What would shift this view materially is a second consecutive strong monthly core CPI print when the [June 10 BLS release](https://www.bls.gov/schedule/news_release/cpi.htm) arrives, which would start bending the smoothed signal upward, or a deterioration in the labor market — rising claims, a soft May payroll report — that pushes the Real Economy Score deeper negative; either alone nudges stress toward Watch, and both together could push it toward the 3–5 range and begin eroding the macro buffer.
