The stress score dropped -2.69 this week to +0.53, landing firmly in Calm, driven almost entirely by [June's CPI print](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/14/consumer-price-index-inflation-report-june-2026.html) resolving last week's central worry: not only did energy pull headline inflation down, but services softened too. Core inflation came in flat on the month and 2.6% year-over-year against a 2.9% consensus, with shelter rising just 0.1%, transportation services falling 0.3%, and apparel and medical care both decelerating — a breadth of cooling that pushed the inflation direction reading to +0.00 and sent the macro buffer to +0.47, just below the full-strength ceiling of +0.50. Growth gave a partial lift: [weekly jobless claims fell to 208,000, a 10-week low](https://www.usnews.com/news/business/articles/2026-07-16/us-weekly-unemployment-claims-fall-to-208-000-fewest-in-10-weeks), and [GDPNow's Q2 tracking has risen to roughly 1.7%](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow) from the ~1.3% flagged last week, but the Real Economy Score is still -0.71 because June's 57,000 payroll miss keeps the labor component below zero and the hard-data recovery has not caught up to the survey optimism. The residual risk is what a [New York Fed study from July 8](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2026/07/more-tariff-pass-through-is-in-the-pipeline/) spelled out plainly: 47% of service firms and 44% of manufacturers that directly paid tariffs still plan further price increases, many of them held back only by contracts that haven't yet expired, which means June's services calm may reflect timing rather than resolution. The two things that would change this view are the July 30 advance Q2 GDP — where a print well below 1.7% would deepen the growth deficit already dragging the Real Economy Score — and the July CPI due August 12, where services re-acceleration would flip the inflation direction positive and rebuild stress quickly.
