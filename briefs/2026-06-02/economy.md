At 1.3, the macro stress score is in its calmest bucket, and the MRMI macro buffer is nearly intact at 0.44 out of a possible 0.5 — meaning the economy side is providing a clear tailwind for allocation. But the score has climbed 0.46 over the past seven days, the fastest single-week move in recent readings, so the direction matters even if the absolute level stays tame. The culprit is growth, not inflation: the Real Economy Score dropped to -1.1 this week as the Atlanta Fed's [Q2 GDPNow estimate slid to 3.0%](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow/current-and-past-gdpnow-commentaries) from 4.3% just two weeks prior, driven by softer consumer spending and net export reads feeding into the model. Inflation isn't compounding the problem — [core CPI is running at 2.74% year-over-year](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm) and the six-month inflation direction is essentially flat at +0.04 pp, so there is no accelerating price pressure to work alongside the growth softness. That separation is what keeps stress calm: the score moves slowly when only one axis fires, but runs fastest when growth weakens and inflation speeds up at the same time, and right now those two aren't pulling together. One genuine counterweight is [ISM Manufacturing jumping to 54 in May](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/manufacturing-pmi-at-54-may-2026-ism-manufacturing-pmi-report-302786165.html) — its strongest print since May 2022 — suggesting the industrial sector has real underlying demand that could stabilize the GDPNow trajectory in the weeks ahead. The stress score moves to Watch if two things happen together: [May payrolls on June 5](https://www.kiplinger.com/investing/economy/jobs-report-may-2026-what-to-expect) come in near or below the 80k consensus (already weak) and [May CPI on June 10](https://www.bls.gov/cpi/) shows core re-accelerating, because that would finally put both axes pushing stress in the same direction.
