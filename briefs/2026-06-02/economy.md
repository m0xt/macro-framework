The economy pillar's stress score sits at 1.3 — firmly in the Calm range — but it has risen almost half a point in the past week, and the driver is a deteriorating growth picture rather than an inflation shock. The real economy score has slipped to -1.1: the [Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow) cut its Q2 estimate to 3.0% on June 1 after spending most of May above 4%, [April payrolls printed 115K](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm) with unemployment holding at 4.3%, and [April personal income dipped while spending rose 0.5%](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/personal-income-and-outlays-april-2026) — pushing the personal saving rate down to 2.6%, a sign consumers are spending through their buffers rather than out of income strength. Inflation is adding very little pressure for now: core CPI sits at 2.74% year-over-year and the six-month change in direction is essentially flat at +0.04 percentage points, so price acceleration is not compounding the growth weakness — which is what keeps the overall stress score as low as it is. There is an important wrinkle, though: the [May ISM manufacturing PMI](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/manufacturing-pmi-at-54-may-2026-ism-manufacturing-pmi-report-302786165.html) jumped to 54.0, its best read since 2022, likely reflecting front-running demand ahead of tariff-driven supply disruption, but input prices in that same survey stayed near 82 — a level that has historically been a leading indicator for goods inflation reaccelerating with a lag of a few months. Because stress remains calm and inflation direction has not yet turned sharply upward, the macro buffer feeding MRMI holds near full strength at 0.44 (against a 0.5 maximum), so the framework is not penalizing the regime signal today. That changes if the May jobs report on June 5 misses meaningfully — [consensus is already running soft at roughly 85–89K](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-30/us-jobs-report-set-to-reveal-solid-growth-steady-unemployment-rate) — or if the May CPI print on June 10 shows [core CPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm) accelerating above April's 2.8%, as that combination would simultaneously pull the real economy score lower and push the inflation direction axis higher, moving stress toward the Watch zone and beginning to erode the MRMI buffer.
