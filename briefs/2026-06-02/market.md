MMI is solidly green at +0.559, and most of the weekly improvement came from financial conditions snapping back after bond-market stress earlier in May. Equity volatility (VIX at 16.2) and rate volatility (MOVE at 73) are both near their lowest levels of the year, and [high-yield credit spreads at 274 basis points](https://streetstats.finance/rates/corporates) are extraordinarily tight — the corporate bond market is effectively saying defaults are not a concern, which is a clean green signal for momentum. Breadth — measuring how widely the rally is shared beyond a few megacap leaders — remains very elevated at 0.853, but has softened slightly over the week as the latest leg was disproportionately powered by semiconductors: [Micron crossed the $1 trillion market cap mark on May 26](https://cryptodaily.co.uk/2026/05/sp500-breadth-beyond-big-tech), and the index's RSI has pushed above 74, leaving the question of whether cyclicals and industrials can maintain their pace. The growth impulse sub-index is the softest of the three at +0.250, but it caught a sharp single-day boost today after [copper surged 3.7% to $6.67 per pound](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/copper-s-giant-tariff-trade-is-back-and-squeezing-global-market) — copper is a cyclical bellwether in the GII framework — driven by AI data-center demand, a 23-year low in Chilean mine output, and tariff front-running ahead of a US import decision due June 30. The 2s/10s yield curve sits at a positive 47 basis points (4.45% vs 3.98%), which adds another green tick for the growth impulse, but the broader macro backdrop is more complicated: [CPI printed 3.8% in April with PPI surging 6%](https://finance.yahoo.com/economy/article/hot-cpi-report-likely-to-put-fed-on-guard-for-longer-lasting-inflation-meanwhile-rate-hike-odds-are-rising-141806600.html), and markets are now pricing roughly a one-in-three chance of a Fed hike before year-end under new Chair Kevin Warsh, whose first FOMC meeting is June 17. The setup reverses quickly if Warsh signals a genuine tightening bias — that would push rate volatility and credit spreads higher in short order, reversing the financial-conditions gains that are currently doing the heavy lifting for MMI.
