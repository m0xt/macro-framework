MMI at +0.559 keeps the dashboard at LONG, and financial conditions are doing the most work this week. All three components — equity volatility, rate volatility, and credit spreads — are pointing the same direction: calm. VIX sits at 16.20, the MOVE index (which measures expected swings in US Treasuries) is at 73.33, and [high-yield credit spreads have compressed to around 274 basis points](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/bofa-merrill-lynch-us-high-yield-option-adjusted-spread-fed-data.html), near multi-year tights, meaning corporate bond investors are not demanding much compensation for risk. That calm has a fundamental anchor: [S&P 500 earnings grew nearly 29% year-over-year](https://www.schwab.com/learn/story/stock-market-update-open), well above expectations, driving the index to fresh record highs this week on the back of a surge in Nvidia and AI-adjacent names. The tension worth watching is that breadth — the share of stocks actually participating in the rally — has slipped for the second week running (7-day: −0.060), landing at 0.853 in absolute terms but with [only about half of S&P 500 members trading above their 50-day moving average](https://cryptodaily.co.uk/2026/05/sp500-breadth-beyond-big-tech), a sign the rally is getting narrower, not broader. The Growth Impulse score has also softened over seven days (−0.069), with [copper prices chopping on inflation fears and uneven Chinese industrial demand](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/21/copper-industrial-metals-inflation-bond-yields.html) even as today's one-day jump (+0.116) shows the impulse hasn't rolled over. The framework stays bullish so long as credit holds and vol stays contained; what flips that read is breadth continuing to hollow out into a narrower tech trade, HY spreads drifting back above 300 bps, or a MOVE pickup that turns the bond market into a headwind rather than a neutral.
