The dashboard is sitting at CAUTION (75% exposure) with MRMI at +0.245 — just five hundredths of a point below the threshold where it would flip to LONG — and the gap between what markets are pricing and what the economy is reporting explains precisely why it hasn't crossed over. Market momentum is at its strongest reading of the year, driven by [HY credit spreads near multi-year lows](https://www.macrotrends.net/3006/high-yield-spread), a calm VIX, and equity breadth still above 0.85; that signal alone would be enough to push the posture to full exposure, but the macro buffer has quietly shed ground this week as [GDPNow fell from 4.3% to 3.0%](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow/current-and-past-gdpnow-commentaries) in under two weeks, trimming the buffer from its maximum and holding MRMI just short of the line. The stress side of the economy picture is not the problem — [core CPI running flat in direction at 2.74%](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm) means growth and inflation aren't compounding each other yet — but markets are priced as though growth is fine, and [274-basis-point HY spreads](https://www.macrotrends.net/3006/high-yield-spread) leave almost no buffer if the economy data catches up to the GDPNow drift. The two data releases that matter most are [May payrolls on June 5](https://www.kiplinger.com/investing/economy/jobs-report-may-2026-what-to-expect) and [May CPI on June 10](https://www.bls.gov/cpi/): a payrolls beat would likely close the last gap to LONG by stabilizing the growth read, while a miss near the 80,000 consensus would deepen the macro buffer drag and give credit markets a reason to widen, hitting MMI from the other direction simultaneously. The scenario that pushes MRMI meaningfully lower is the one both pillars flag in unison — payrolls disappoint and CPI re-accelerates — because that is the only combination that gets growth and inflation pulling stress in the same direction at once.
