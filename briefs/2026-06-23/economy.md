Despite the [FOMC delivering exactly the hawkish signal the previous brief flagged as a risk](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260617a.htm) — nine of eighteen members now projecting at least one rate hike before year-end, and [the Fed's own year-end PCE inflation forecast raised by a full 0.9 points to 3.6%](https://www.stocktitan.net/articles/fed-rate-decision-june-17-2026) — the macro stress score sits at +1.91 (7d +0.05), still deep in the Calm band, and the macro buffer feeding MRMI barely moved, slipping from +0.41 last week to +0.40. That counterintuitive result reflects how the dashboard is wired: stress rises on actual core CPI direction and real-economy conditions, not on the Fed's stated intentions, and both inputs were essentially flat — Inflation Direction held at +0.30 pp and the Real Economy Score edged marginally weaker to -0.47. Growth looks fine for now: [Atlanta Fed GDPNow ticked back to 3.04% for Q2](https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/data/gdpnow) as of June 17 and May retail sales came in strong at +0.9% month-over-month, so the spending picture doesn't support a stress repricing on the growth side. On inflation, the tariff pass-through story is actually more ambiguous than feared: [Minneapolis Fed analysis](https://www.minneapolisfed.org/article/2026/tariffs-cant-explain-rising-goods-inflation) shows core goods prices fell in May — vehicles, furniture, and apparel all down — and that deflation in traded goods is what kept core CPI YoY anchored at 2.90% and the Inflation Direction axis from drifting further positive. The gap between the Fed projecting 3.6% PCE by year-end and the dashboard sitting comfortably in Calm is the central tension now: the framework isn't wrong, it's waiting for the data to confirm or refute the Fed's concern, and the [May PCE report due June 25](https://www.bea.gov/data/personal-consumption-expenditures-price-index) is the first real test — if core PCE pushes meaningfully above its current 3.3% annual rate and core goods stop falling, the Inflation Direction axis will start climbing and buffer erosion will pick up pace.
