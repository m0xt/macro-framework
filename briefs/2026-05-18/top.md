MRMI is holding a LONG signal at +0.504, but the architecture of that score is as important as the number: the macro buffer is contributing nearly everything at +0.966 while MMI has collapsed to +0.038 — a split that wide between the two pillars is a warning, not a confirmation. [Moody's downgrade of U.S. sovereign debt to Aa1 on May 16](https://ratings.moodys.com/ratings-news/443154) is the thread that ties both stories together: the same structural deficit trajectory the agency cited is what's driving the fiscal bear steepening that pushed 10-year yields toward 4.61% and turned GII negative in the market pillar, and it's the same arithmetic underlying the CBO's $3.4 trillion cost estimate for the "One Big Beautiful Bill" that the economy pillar identifies as a forward pressure on term premium. The economy's read is similarly unresolved: [headline CPI at 3.8%](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/12/cpi-inflation-april-2026-.html) looks alarming in isolation, but the inflation direction delta is barely positive and the composition — energy-led, services-sticky, tariff pass-through absent — means trend acceleration hasn't compounded yet, while the real economy score at −0.785 flags genuine income softness beneath the nominal spending resilience. What's keeping the MRMI intact is the FinCon firewall: [HY OAS near 280 basis points](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BAMLH0A0HYM2) and MOVE at 79.87 tell you credit markets have not yet priced what Moody's just formalized, and as long as that holds, the macro buffer stays hard-clipped near full strength. The dominant risk to the LONG is the gap closing in the wrong direction — either the Moody's catalyst migrates from the rates market into credit spreads and compresses FinCon, or services inflation persists into the May print and tips the inflation delta meaningfully positive just as the Sahm indicator is approaching its 0.5pp threshold, pushing stress intensity off the floor and beginning to erode the buffer. The [90-day US-China tariff truce](https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/customs-import-compliance-blog/us-china-tariff-truce-90-day-pause-begins-may-14) is the modest counterweight on both sides — it removes the near-term goods inflation tail and the sharpest demand-destruction scenario — but at a 30% residual rate it remains a headwind to the global growth signals GII needs to recover. The regime stays LONG, but the signal is resting almost entirely on FinCon holding a line that fiscal reality is actively pressuring.
