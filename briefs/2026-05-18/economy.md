The macro stress pressure score sits modestly above its historical neutral — raw reading of 0.432 against a ~0.25 baseline, placing it roughly in the upper half of its observed range and pointing toward stress that is building but not yet unusual — with the MRMI buffer still running at nearly full strength (+0.966) because the hard-clipped trigger that actually erodes the regime signal remains close to zero. The two axes are pulling in opposite directions: the real economy score at −0.785 is the heavier weight, reflecting meaningful softness across the labor and income data even as GDPNow's Q2 read of roughly +4% annualized keeps the composite from deteriorating further. The inflation direction delta — the 6-month change in core CPI — is a barely-positive +0.043 pp, meaning the acceleration in prices is not yet strong enough on a trend basis to compound aggressively against the growth weakness; core CPI YoY sits at 2.74%, above the Fed's target but not the story the headline suggests.

The April [CPI print](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm) released Tuesday was the week's defining data point: headline jumped to [3.8% YoY](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/12/cpi-inflation-april-2026-.html), the highest since May 2023, but the composition matters enormously for how to read it through the framework — energy prices, specifically gasoline up 28.4% annually, drove more than 40% of the monthly gain, and [core goods were essentially flat (+0.03% MoM)](https://verifiedinvesting.com/blogs/us-economic-metrics/us-cpi-april-2026-core-doubles-services-not-tariffs), meaning tariff pass-through into merchandise has not materialized in size. The acceleration in core CPI came instead from services — shelter rebounded 0.6% on the month partly due to a statistical distortion from last October's government shutdown, and airline fares surged — categories that are stickier but also less directly tied to the tariff regime. Real average hourly wages [fell 0.3% annually](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/12/cpi-inflation-april-2026-.html), which is the more consequential signal for the growth axis: income erosion is exactly what keeps the real economy score depressed even when headline spending ([retail sales +0.5% in April, control group +0.5%](https://kpmg.com/us/en/articles/2026/april-2026-retail-sales.html)) holds up on a nominal basis.

The macro-relevant development that complicates the outlook in both directions is the [US-China 90-day tariff truce](https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/customs-import-compliance-blog/us-china-tariff-truce-90-day-pause-begins-may-14) that took effect May 14 — cutting the US rate on Chinese goods from 145% to 30% — which simultaneously reduces the near-term goods inflation tail risk that could have pushed the inflation direction delta sharply positive, and removes the most acute demand-destruction scenario that was weighing on the growth side. The pressure score would move materially higher if shelter and services inflation persist into May data, if the Sahm indicator — currently approaching but not yet at its 0.5 pp threshold — crosses the line, or if energy prices reignite a second headline shock that begins bleeding into longer-run inflation expectations; for now the framework's read is elevated vigilance, not an active stress regime.
