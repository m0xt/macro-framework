The MMI is clinging to marginally positive territory at +0.038, but the 504-point seven-day drawdown tells the real story: all three sub-components deteriorated together over the week, and the composite is effectively at stall speed. The dominant drag is GII, which slipped into negative territory at −0.064, driven primarily by the dollar's continued elevation — the broad nominal dollar index near 118 acts as a persistent tax on cross-asset global growth signals, compressing commodity impulse and weighing on the carry that typically supports credit-spread compression. Simultaneously, the 10-year yield pushed toward [4.61% by week's end](https://eciks.org/4297-71780-10-year-treasury-yield-rises-to-4-61-up-14-basis-points-from-may-14) — not the growth-driven steepening that supports GII, but a fiscal-driven bear steepening tied to the [Congressional Budget Office's estimate](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/13/traders-predict-trump-will-make-major-announcements-during-china-trip.html) that the "One Big Beautiful Bill" adds roughly $3.4 trillion to federal debt, which markets are beginning to price as structurally higher term premium. Breadth remains the most constructive component at +0.113, but its seven-day decline of −0.669 is the sharpest of the three, reflecting a fading of the cyclical rotation that had front-run a [Trump-Xi trade truce extension](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/14/trump-xi-summit-us-china-trade-taiwan-iran-nvidia.html) — Energy, Materials, and Industrials led early in the week but lost momentum as the fiscal and dollar overhang reasserted itself. FinCon is the one pillar still clearly supported by data: [HY OAS at 280 basis points](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BAMLH0A0HYM2) is near multi-year tights, MOVE at 79.87 signals the bond market is not panicking despite the yield move, and VIX at 18.35 keeps equity vol firmly in the neutral range — the credit market, in short, is refusing to confirm the stress implied by the GII's negative read. The key divergence to watch is precisely this gap between a benign FinCon and a deteriorating GII: historically that gap closes either through credit spreads eventually repricing to reflect fiscal tail risk or through the dollar softening and relieving global growth pressure — which direction it closes will determine whether the MMI recovers toward a clear LONG signal or continues eroding toward CASH.
